


The Russian Revolution of 1917 was a monumental political turning point that dismantled the centuries-old Romanov monarchy and led to the creation of the Soviet Union. Sparked by widespread wartime starvation, economic collapse, and severe casualties during World War I, the revolution unfolded in two distinct phases. In March 1917 (the February Revolution), popular protests forced Tsar Nicholas II to abdicate, replacing the monarchy with a weak Provisional Government. By November 1917 (the October Revolution), Vladimir Lenin and his radical Bolshevik party capitalized on public discontent with promises of "Peace, Land, and Bread," overthrowing the government in a swift coup to establish the world's first communist state.


The Indian Independence Movement was a decades-long struggle that ultimately ended nearly two centuries of British colonial rule in 1947. Sparked by growing resistance to economic exploitation and political oppression, the movement was defined by a powerful mix of peaceful mass protests and radical revolutionary actions. Leaders like Mahatma Gandhi united millions through non-violent civil disobedience, boycotts, and hunger strikes, while revolutionaries like Bhagat Singh, Subhas Chandra Bose, and Chandrashekhar Azad pursued armed resistance to actively disrupt British control. Heavily pressured by this widespread domestic resistance and financially drained by World War II, Great Britain finally granted independence on August 15, 1947, resulting in the freedom of India and its painful partition into two separate nations, India and Pakistan.
